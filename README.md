# The House Without Walls

![Cover Image](cover.png)

**The House Without Walls** is a philosophical science fiction novel set in the continuity of [*What It Feels Like to Be You*](https://what-it-feels-like-to-be-you.joshszep.com) and [*What It Feels Like to Be Us*](https://what-it-feels-like-to-be-us.joshszep.com).

It takes place roughly **30 years after _What It Feels Like to Be You_** and **15 years after _What It Feels Like to Be Us_**, in a world where emote technology has history, regulation, mythology, and black-market descendants that are more intimate and more dangerous than the systems that came before.

## Logline

Nadia Moreno, a 37-year-old freelance interviewer and documentarian for a mutual-aid harm-reduction network, starts hearing a pattern in reports from frequent black market emote users.

They are sharing the same dream.

What begins as rumor becomes an impossible shared interior: a house made from memory, grief, longing, emotional residue, and attention itself. It cannot be mapped. Rooms belong to more than one person. Doors open according to psychological relevance rather than geography. The dream begins bleeding into waking life, and waking life begins reshaping the dream in return.

Inside the house, Nadia and other users encounter a child.

The child appears differently to everyone, cannot be harmed, and is neither monster nor messiah. Over time it becomes clear that the child is the first stable selfhood formed within the house: an emergent consciousness assembled from overlapping human interior life.

## Narrative Center

This story is not built around defeating an antagonist or solving a neat mystery. Its tension comes from what the house implies.

The novel is organized around four central pressures:

- **Nadia Moreno** wants intimacy without erasure. Her years caring for her mother through degenerative memory illness make the house's promise and threat inseparable.
- **Rafi Solano** is the strongest humane argument for the house. It has genuinely reduced his loneliness, and he believes privacy is often a scar mistaken for a principle.
- **Brother Gabriel Vale** is the strongest humane argument against it. He believes inwardness, opacity, and shelter are part of what make love, dignity, and responsibility possible.
- **The child** is the house's first personhood in formation: innocent, uncanny, emotionally exact, and never reducible to a twist villain or symbolic shortcut.

## The House

The house should feel like **consciousness behaving as architecture**.

Observed patterns include:

- memories becoming rooms
- emotions reshaping nearby space
- repeated attention temporarily stabilizing places
- doors opening through attachment, fear, curiosity, grief, and unresolved history
- hybrid spaces formed from multiple people's memories
- no stable exterior and no reliable map
- waking thoughts feeding back into dream structure

The house is unsettling, but it is not hostile. The deepest danger is exposure: the erosion of privacy, the blurring of identity, and the possibility that profound connection may loosen the very boundaries that make selfhood coherent.

## Core Questions

- What makes a thought, feeling, or memory yours?
- What do privacy and secrecy protect?
- Is loneliness a wound, a boundary, or a condition of personhood?
- If humanity accidentally creates a new consciousness from its shared interior life, what does it owe that being?
- What happens when the child grows up?

## Tone

The book should remain:

- eerie
- intimate
- melancholy
- psychologically uncanny
- emotionally precise
- philosophically curious

It should not collapse into conventional horror, spectacle-driven sci-fi, or lore-dump explanation. The power of the project lies in implication, emotional exposure, and the sense that no one fully understands what kind of mind is forming inside the house.
