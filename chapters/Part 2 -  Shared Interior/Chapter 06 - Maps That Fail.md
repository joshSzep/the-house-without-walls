# Chapter 06: Maps That Fail

The first time they tried to map the house, Nadia brought a roll of butcher paper and a handful of colored markers, as if the right tools could make the impossible less so. Rafi arrived late, apologizing with a half-smile and a thermos of coffee, and Gabriel came only after Nadia promised the meeting would not become a séance. They met in the same print cooperative room, the table still marked by magenta ghosts and the memory of last week's confessions.

Jae was already sketching when Nadia arrived, his lines hesitant, as if afraid the paper might reject what he drew. Pilar sat beside him, arms folded, watching the door as if expecting the house itself to walk in. Martin had not come. Nadia suspected he would not return for a while.

They began by drawing what they remembered: the staircase, the landing, the kitchen, the hallway of doors. Each person described their route, and Nadia tried to fit the pieces together, but the lines refused to meet. Jae's staircase turned left where Pilar's turned right. Nadia's kitchen window looked onto a hallway that, in Rafi's version, led to a garden no one else had seen. The more they tried to agree, the more the house slipped away from them, like a word repeated until it lost meaning.

"Maybe it's not supposed to fit," Jae said, erasing a wall for the third time. "Maybe it's like those dreams where you know the rooms are connected, but you can't walk them in order."

Gabriel, who had been silent, finally spoke. "Or maybe the house isn't a place at all. Maybe it's a process."

Rafi nodded, but his eyes were on Nadia. "Or maybe it's both."

They tried again, this time using colors for each person's route. The paper became a tangle of lines, intersections that made sense only for a moment before dissolving. Pilar traced her path to the kitchen, then stopped. "It was here," she said, tapping the paper. "But the next time, the door was on the other side."

Nadia felt frustration rising, but underneath it, something else: a strange relief. The house would not let itself be solved. It would not become a diagram. She realized she had been afraid it might.

They compared notes on the rules they thought they had discovered. Attention could stabilize a room, but only for a while. Emotion could distort a place, making it larger or smaller, brighter or dimmer. Doors responded to need, not geography. The more they tried to fix the house in place, the more it responded by shifting.

Gabriel grew more insistent as the meeting went on. "There are things we shouldn't try to master," he said. "Some boundaries exist for a reason. If you make everything permeable, you lose the self."

Rafi countered, "Or you find out the self was never as solid as you thought."

Nadia listened, caught between them. She wanted to believe in the possibility of mapping, of making the house safe through understanding. But she could not ignore the evidence: every attempt to pin the house down made it stranger, more alive, more resistant.

As the meeting ended, Nadia looked at the paper covered in lines, arrows, and half-erased rooms. It looked less like a map and more like a record of longing. She rolled it up and tucked it under her arm, unsure whether she would ever unroll it again.

That night, in the house, she found herself in a corridor she did not recognize. The walls were hung with photographs, some familiar, some not. She paused before one: a picture of herself as a child, standing in a kitchen that was not her own. In the reflection of the window, she saw someone else watching her—a face she almost recognized, but not quite.

She woke with the sense that the house had answered her attempt at mastery with a new question. Not where is this, but who is this for?
