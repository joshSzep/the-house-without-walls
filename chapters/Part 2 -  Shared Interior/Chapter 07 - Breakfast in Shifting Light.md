# Chapter 07: Breakfast in Shifting Light

Nadia woke with the memory of a corridor that did not belong to her, the aftertaste of someone else's longing still sharp in her mouth. She sat up, uncertain whether she had slept at all, and found herself already thinking about the house—not as a puzzle, but as a place she would soon return to, whether she chose to or not.

The next time she entered, it was morning in the house. Or something like morning: a pale, shifting light filtered through curtains that did not match, illuminating a kitchen table set for breakfast. The table was too large for the room, crowded with mismatched bowls, a chipped mug, a plate of toast gone cold. Nadia stood in the doorway, unsure whether she was intruding or expected.

A child sat on the floor, legs crossed, drawing with a stub of blue crayon on the back of an envelope. The child looked up, not startled, not shy—simply present. Their face was neither familiar nor strange, and Nadia could not have said whether they were a boy or a girl, or how old they might be. The child’s hair caught the light in a way that made it impossible to decide its color.

Nadia hesitated. "Is this your kitchen?"

The child considered, then shook their head. "It's just where I am now."

Nadia stepped inside, careful not to disturb the arrangement of bowls and cups. She sat at the table, her hands folded in her lap. The child returned to drawing, filling the envelope with looping lines and small, careful shapes.

"Do you come here often?" Nadia asked.

The child shrugged. "I come when someone is here. Or when I want to see if someone will be."

Nadia watched the child for a long moment. The silence was not uncomfortable. It was attentive, as if the room itself was listening. She noticed the details: the way the light shifted across the table, the faint smell of oranges, the sound of water running somewhere out of sight.

"What are you drawing?" she asked.

The child turned the envelope so Nadia could see. It was not a picture of the house, or of any room she recognized. It was a tangle of lines, some looping, some straight, intersecting and diverging. "It's how I know where I am," the child said. "Sometimes it changes."

Nadia nodded, feeling the truth of that settle in her chest. "Does it ever stay the same?"

"Only when someone else is here," the child said. "Then it holds for a while."

They sat together in the shifting light, the ordinary strangeness of breakfast stretching between them. Nadia felt no fear, only the uncanny certainty that she was being met, not merely observed. The child looked at her with a calmness that was neither innocence nor wisdom, but something older and more precise.

"Are you lonely?" Nadia asked, surprised by her own voice.

The child smiled, small and unhurried. "Not right now."

Nadia closed her eyes for a moment, letting the quiet fill her. When she opened them again, the child was still there, drawing, the light moving across the floor in slow, impossible patterns.

She woke with the sense that she had not left the house so much as been allowed to carry a piece of its morning with her. The feeling lingered, gentle and unsettling, as if the boundary between her and the house had grown thinner, more permeable, and the child’s presence was now a question she could not stop asking.
