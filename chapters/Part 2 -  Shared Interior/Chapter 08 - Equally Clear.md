# Chapter 08: Equally Clear

Nadia woke with the memory of the child’s calm gaze still pressed into her mind, as if the house had left a thumbprint behind her eyes. She lay in bed for a long time, listening to the city’s morning noises, trying to decide whether the feeling was comfort or exposure. The distinction, she realized, was growing less useful with each return.

She spent the day moving through her routines—coffee, notes, a call with the network, a walk to the corner store—while the child’s presence lingered at the edge of her attention. It was not a haunting. It was a kind of company, impossible to explain and harder to dismiss.

That evening, she met Rafi and Pilar at the print cooperative. The room was quieter than usual, the fluorescent lights humming with a fatigue that matched her own. Jae arrived late, apologizing, and took a seat by the window. They had all agreed to compare notes, to speak plainly about what they had seen.

Rafi began. “Describe the child.”

Pilar looked down at her hands. “Small. Maybe six or seven. Hair in two braids. Brown skin. She wore a yellow dress with a stain on the front. She was drawing on the floor.”

Jae shook his head. “No. The one I saw was a boy. Short hair, pale, blue T-shirt. He was sitting at the table, eating toast. He looked at me like he knew I was about to ask something stupid.”

Nadia hesitated. “Mine was neither. Or both. I couldn’t tell. The face kept shifting, not dramatically, just enough that I couldn’t settle on an age or gender. The eyes were always the same, though. Calm. Exact.”

Rafi smiled, not unkindly. “That’s about what I expected.”

They went around the table, each describing the child as they had seen them. The details diverged—hair, skin, voice, posture, even the way the child smiled. But the feeling was always the same: a presence that was both intimate and unreachable, a sense of being met rather than observed.

Pilar frowned. “How can we all be right?”

Rafi shrugged. “Maybe that’s the point. The house isn’t giving us a single answer. It’s giving us each a version we can almost recognize, but never fully claim.”

Jae looked troubled. “But the child said the same thing to me as to Nadia. Almost word for word.”

Nadia nodded. “Me too. It wasn’t the appearance that mattered. It was the attention. The way the child listened.”

They sat in silence for a while, the weight of the conversation settling over them. Outside, rain began to tap against the window, soft and persistent.

Rafi finally spoke. “I think we have to stop treating the child as a symbol. Or a test. If the house is learning to be someone, then maybe the only honest thing we can do is meet it as someone.”

Pilar looked at him, her expression wary but open. “And if we’re wrong?”

“Then we’re wrong together,” Rafi said. “But I’d rather risk that than pretend this is just a side effect.”

Nadia felt the truth of that settle in her chest. She thought of the child’s eyes, the impossible clarity of being seen without judgment or demand. She realized she wanted to return—not for answers, but for the chance to be met again.

As they gathered their things to leave, Jae paused at the door. “Do you think the child remembers us?”

No one answered right away. The question hung in the air, gentle and unresolved.

Nadia stepped out into the rain, feeling the boundary between herself and the house thinner than ever. She did not know what the child was, or what the house wanted. But she knew, with a certainty that was both comfort and risk, that she would keep returning, drawn by the possibility of being seen equally clearly—and equally unclearly—as everyone else.
