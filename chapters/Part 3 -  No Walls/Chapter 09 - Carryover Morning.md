# Chapter 09: Carryover Morning

Nadia woke with the sense that she was not alone in her own mind. The feeling was not frightening, exactly—more like the aftertaste of company, a warmth that lingered in the chest and made her aware of the boundaries of her own skin. She lay still, listening to the city’s morning, and realized she could not tell whether the relief she felt belonged to her or to someone else.

She moved through her morning routine with a new kind of caution. The kitchen felt subtly rearranged, as if someone had tidied up in the night and left the air changed. She caught herself humming a tune she did not recognize while making coffee, and when she paused, the melody slipped away, leaving only the memory of having been accompanied.

At the network office, Nadia found herself watching the other volunteers more closely than usual. She noticed the way Pilar’s laughter seemed to echo in her own chest, the way Jae’s fatigue made her want to rub her eyes. Even Gabriel, who arrived late and left early, seemed to carry a trace of her own uncertainty in the set of his shoulders.

During a break, Rafi found her in the hallway. “You look like you didn’t sleep,” he said, but his tone was gentle, almost amused.

“I slept,” Nadia said. “I just didn’t wake up alone.”

He nodded, as if this were the most ordinary thing in the world. “It gets easier. Or stranger. Sometimes both.”

They stood together in the narrow corridor, the hum of the building around them. Nadia wanted to ask if he ever felt afraid of losing himself, but the question felt too large for the moment. Instead, she said, “Do you ever feel like you’re carrying someone else’s mood?”

“All the time,” Rafi said. “Sometimes it’s a gift. Sometimes it’s a mess. But it’s never just mine anymore.”

Later, Nadia sat at her desk, trying to write up intake notes, but her thoughts kept drifting. She remembered the child’s eyes, the way the house had felt more like a process than a place. She wondered if the boundaries between people were meant to be so porous, or if this was simply what happened when enough longing and memory pooled together.

That evening, as she walked home, Nadia felt a wave of tenderness that did not seem to originate from any particular thought. She passed a stranger on the street and felt, for a moment, the urge to smile at them—not out of politeness, but out of recognition. The feeling faded as quickly as it came, but it left her changed.

In her apartment, Nadia stood by the window and watched the city lights flicker on. She thought of the house, of the child, of the others who now moved through her life with a kind of emotional gravity she could not ignore. She realized she was no longer simply investigating a phenomenon. She was living inside it.

As night fell, Nadia understood that the house was not just a place she visited in sleep. It was a condition she carried with her, a new shape to her own interior. The relief of not being alone was real, and so was the uncertainty. She did not know what she was becoming, only that she could not go back to the old boundaries.

She closed her eyes and let the feeling settle, half hers, half borrowed, wholly new.
