# Chapter 11: A Question No One Taught

Nadia entered the house with a sense of quiet readiness, as if she had been rehearsing for this visit all day without knowing it. The corridor was familiar, but the light was different—softer, almost expectant. She followed the sound of quiet movement to a room she had not seen before: a small kitchen, sunlight pooling on the floor, the air thick with the scent of oranges and something like rain.

The child was there, sitting at the table, feet swinging above the floor. They looked up as Nadia entered, their face composed of features she could not quite hold in memory. The child smiled, not with welcome or challenge, but with a kind of gentle precision.

Nadia sat across from them. For a moment, neither spoke. The silence was not empty. It was attentive, as if the house itself were listening.

The child set down a spoon and asked, “What part of you disappears when another person knows it?”

The question landed in the room like a change in weather. Nadia felt her breath catch. She tried to answer, but the words would not come. She realized she had never thought to ask herself that question, let alone answer it.

She looked at the child, searching for a clue in their expression, but the child only waited, patient and unhurried.

Nadia tried to speak. “I don’t know. Maybe nothing. Maybe everything. Maybe the part that needs to be hidden.”

The child tilted their head. “Why does it need to be hidden?”

Nadia hesitated. “Because some things feel too fragile to survive being seen. Or maybe because if someone else knows them, they stop being only mine.”

The child nodded, as if this were a reasonable answer. “Does that make you less real, or more?”

Nadia closed her eyes. She thought of her mother, of the years spent trying to keep memory from dissolving, of the longing to be known without being erased. “I don’t know,” she said softly. “Sometimes I think it’s both.”

The child smiled again, a small, precise gesture. “I think that’s what the house is learning.”

They sat together in the shifting light, the question lingering between them. Nadia felt the boundaries of her self become porous, not in a way that frightened her, but in a way that made her aware of how much she had always wanted to be met, not just observed.

When she woke, the question remained. It echoed through her day, surfacing in the smallest moments: a glance from a stranger, a memory triggered by a scent, the way Rafi’s laughter made her feel both more herself and less alone.

That evening, Nadia wrote the question in her notebook: What part of you disappears when another person knows it?

She did not try to answer it. She only let it live there, a horizon she could not yet reach, but could no longer ignore.
