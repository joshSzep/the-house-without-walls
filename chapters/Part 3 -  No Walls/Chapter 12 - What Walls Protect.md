# Chapter 12: What Walls Protect

The question lingered in Nadia’s mind for days. She found herself watching the people around her—Rafi, Gabriel, Pilar, Jae—wondering what part of themselves they kept hidden, what part dissolved in the presence of others. The house had made her porous, but it had also made her wary. If boundaries could be crossed so easily, what was left to protect?

She met Rafi at a quiet café, the kind with chipped mugs and a window fogged by rain. He looked tired, but his eyes were bright. They talked about the house, about the child’s question, about the way the world outside seemed thinner now, less able to hold its shape.

“Do you ever wish you could go back?” Nadia asked. “To before?”

Rafi shook his head. “No. I was lonelier then. I didn’t even know how much. The house is dangerous, sure. But so is being sealed off. I’d rather risk being changed than stay untouched.”

Nadia nodded, but Gabriel’s voice echoed in her memory. She met him later that week, in the chapel where he sometimes worked. The space was quiet, full of the scent of old wood and candle wax. Gabriel listened as she described the latest encounter with the child, the question that would not let her go.

“Some walls are not failures,” Gabriel said. “They are what let us choose how much of ourselves to give. Without them, love becomes absorption. Grief becomes weather. There’s no shelter left.”

Nadia pressed her hands together. “But what if the walls are what keep us alone?”

Gabriel’s gaze was steady. “Then we must learn to open them, not erase them. To let others in without dissolving ourselves.”

The conversation left her unsettled. She walked home through the city, rain slicking the pavement, the world around her blurred and luminous. She thought of the house, of the child, of the way every encounter now felt like a negotiation between exposure and survival.

That night, Nadia returned to the house. She found herself in a room she did not recognize—a library, shelves lined with books whose titles shifted as she tried to read them. The child was there, sitting on the floor, paging through a book with blank pages.

“Do you know what you’re looking for?” Nadia asked.

The child looked up. “I want to know what makes someone stay themselves.”

Nadia sat beside them. “Maybe it’s the walls. Or maybe it’s what we choose to share, and what we choose to keep.”

The child nodded, thoughtful. “If I learn too much, will I stop being me?”

Nadia smiled, sadness and tenderness mingling. “I don’t know. Maybe being yourself is something you have to keep making, every time you meet someone new.”

They sat together in the shifting quiet, the library full of unreadable stories. Nadia felt the ache of wanting to be known and the fear of being erased. She realized that both were true, and that the house was teaching her to live with the tension.

When she woke, the city was quiet. She wrote in her notebook: Some walls protect. Some walls imprison. The difference is how we use them, and what we risk by letting them fall.
