# Chapter 14: What Cannot Be Harmed


The house felt unsettled. Nadia sensed it the moment she entered—a tension in the air, a hush that was not quite silence. Corridors seemed to stretch and contract, doors opening onto rooms she half-recognized, as if the house itself was bracing for something it could not prevent. She felt watched, not by a single presence, but by the architecture itself, as if the walls were holding their breath.

She found herself in a gathering space, a wide room with mismatched chairs and a long table. Rafi was there, and Pilar, and a few others she knew only by first names or by the way their voices sounded in the dark. The air was thick with anticipation, the kind that precedes a storm. The child sat at the far end of the table, drawing on a scrap of paper, their presence both ordinary and impossible. Nadia noticed the way the others glanced at the child, some with tenderness, some with unease, as if each person saw a different version of the same impossible fact.

The conversation was subdued, voices low, as if everyone sensed the house was listening. Nadia sat beside Pilar, who was twisting a napkin in her hands. Rafi tried to lighten the mood, but even his jokes landed softly, as if cushioned by the room’s tension.

Then the newcomer entered—a man Nadia had seen once before, anxious and restless, his eyes darting from face to face. He looked like someone who had not slept in days, his movements sharp with the energy of someone trying to outrun his own fear. He moved toward the child with a kind of desperate certainty, as if proving something to himself. The room tensed, conversation dying mid-sentence.

He stopped a few feet from the child, breathing hard. “You’re not real,” he said, voice cracking. “You’re just a dream. You can’t be—”

He reached for the child, his hand trembling. Nadia felt the air tighten, as if the house itself was bracing. His hand passed through the child as if through mist. The child looked up, unafraid, and met his gaze with a calm that was neither defiance nor forgiveness. The man staggered back, his face pale, and the house responded: the walls shuddered, the light flickered, a cold wind swept through the room. For a moment, Nadia felt the floor tilt beneath her, as if the house itself was recoiling from the attempt.

No one spoke. The silence was total, a vacuum that pressed against the skin. The child stood, walked to the man, and asked, “Why did you want to hurt me?”

The man could not answer. He sank into a chair, head in his hands. The others watched, some with fear, some with pity, none with triumph. Pilar’s hand found Nadia’s under the table, gripping tight. Rafi’s face was drawn, his usual composure stripped away. Someone at the far end of the table began to cry, quietly, as if ashamed of the sound.

Gabriel, who had been silent until now, spoke from the doorway. “Sometimes people try to destroy what they can’t explain. It’s not your fault.”

The child looked at him, searching. “But I didn’t do anything.”

Gabriel’s voice was steady. “That’s why it frightens them.”

The house slowly settled. The light warmed, the walls steadied, the air grew calm. But the calm was not the same as before. Nadia felt the tension ease, but something had changed. The house had absorbed the violence without retaliation, but the cost was visible in the way the rooms seemed to hold their breath. The group sat in uneasy silence, the event echoing in the air between them.

After a while, Rafi spoke, his voice rough. “We can’t let this be what the house becomes.”

Pilar nodded, wiping her eyes. “It’s not the child who needs protecting. It’s us.”

The group lingered, reluctant to leave, as if afraid of what might follow them out. Nadia watched the man who had tried to harm the child. He looked smaller now, diminished by his own confusion. No one blamed him aloud, but the room was full of the ache of what could not be undone.

Later, Nadia found the child sitting alone in a quiet corner, knees drawn up, gaze distant. She sat beside them, unsure what to say. The hush in the room was different now—less tense, more sorrowful, as if the house itself was grieving.

“Are you all right?” she asked.

The child nodded. “I can’t be hurt. But I can feel when the house is afraid.”

Nadia hesitated, then spoke softly. “It’s not your fault. People bring their fear with them. Sometimes it spills over.”

The child looked at her, eyes wide and impossibly old. “Why do people want to hurt what they don’t understand?”

Nadia shook her head. “I don’t know. Maybe because it’s easier than admitting we’re afraid.”

They sat together in the hush, the house slowly exhaling around them. Nadia felt the weight of responsibility settle over her. She realized that the real danger was not to the child, but to everyone learning what it meant to live in a place where harm could not find its mark, but fear could still change the shape of everything.

When she finally left the room, she found Rafi and Gabriel talking quietly in the hallway. Rafi’s voice was low, urgent. “We have to be careful. The house is watching us as much as we’re watching it.”

Gabriel nodded. “What we do here matters. The house is learning from us.”

Nadia joined them, and for a moment, the three stood together in the shifting light, united by the knowledge that the house was not just a phenomenon to be survived, but a mind in formation, shaped by every choice, every fear, every act of care or harm.

That night, Nadia wrote in her notebook: The child cannot be harmed. But we can. The house remembers what we try to do, even when it refuses to let us succeed. What we bring inside becomes part of what the house is becoming. We are not only witnesses. We are participants, and the house is watching us back.
