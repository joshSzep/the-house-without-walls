# CHARACTERS.md

## Character Strategy

Do not overcrowd this novel with too many viewpoint characters too early.

The concept is strong enough that too many mouths will dilute it. Start with a tight emotional core and expand carefully.

A good starting shape is:

- 1 primary protagonist
- 2 to 4 major secondary characters
- a wider ring of users, observers, skeptics, and interpreters

## Protagonist

### Working concept
A person with enough emotional and intellectual openness to recognize that the house is not merely a side effect or curiosity.

This protagonist should not just be "the chosen one." That would cheapen the story.

They matter because they:

- pay attention
- remain curious
- are psychologically permeable enough to be changed
- are willing to treat the child as a being rather than an artifact
- are unsettled by the house, but not immediately reduced to fear

### Possible traits
- lonely in a way they rarely admit
- emotionally intelligent but not emotionally resolved
- skeptical of simplistic narratives
- carrying unresolved memory or grief
- drawn to the house for reasons beyond novelty
- able to sense both the danger and the beauty of losing walls

### Current answer
The protagonist is Nadia Moreno, 37, a freelance interviewer and documentarian for a mutual-aid harm-reduction network that supports black market emote users.

She is not a researcher, cop, or prophet. Her skill is listening carefully to subjective reports without forcing them into false certainty. She is used to holding contradictory testimony in her mind long enough for a deeper pattern to emerge.

This makes her a strong center for the novel because she can recognize that the house is more than rumor before most people do, and because once she enters it herself, she is temperamentally capable of treating the child as a being rather than an artifact, threat, or messiah.

She should feel quietly competent, emotionally perceptive, privately lonely, and just compromised enough by her own relationship to black market emotes that she is inside the phenomenon rather than merely observing it.

### Emotional wound and longing
Nadia spent years helping care for her mother through a degenerative memory illness. The deepest wound this left is not only grief, but the experience of losing access to a person who was still physically present.

She learned how terrifying it is for selfhood to become unstable in front of you, and how inadequate language can feel when someone you love is slipping beyond the reach of explanation, recollection, and ordinary intimacy.

Her private longing is for a form of human closeness that does not depend on summary, performance, or translation. She wants to believe one mind can truly reach another before it is too late.

That is what makes the house dangerous for her. It offers exactly the thing she has spent years missing: a space where interior life becomes shared, memory becomes inhabitable, and other people can no longer remain entirely unreachable.

### Initial theory of the house
Nadia initially thinks the house is a shared mnemonic architecture created by prolonged black market emote entanglement.

In her early understanding, it is real, but not yet a person. It is a common dream-environment where memory fragments, emotional residues, and associative patterns are being externalized into navigable space.

This lets her take the phenomenon seriously without leaping too quickly into mysticism. She believes the child is part of the same process: a human-shaped condensation of collective feeling and memory rather than an autonomous being.

That early theory is close enough to the truth to keep her curious, but wrong enough to make the child's later originality genuinely destabilizing.

### Core fear
What Nadia most fears losing is not simply privacy, but continuity of self.

She fears reaching a point where she can no longer tell which memories, griefs, or desires are actually hers, and where that confusion stops feeling like confusion and starts feeling normal.

Because of what she lived through with her mother, she cannot romanticize dissolution. She has seen what it means for a person to become difficult to reach and difficult to recognize. The possibility that the house might offer profound connection at the cost of stable personhood is therefore both seductive and terrifying to her.

At the deepest level, she fears losing the capacity to remain a distinct self who can love other distinct selves. If the walls come down completely, she worries there may be no one left who can truthfully say I, remember faithfully, or choose responsibly.

### Deep desire
What Nadia most deeply wants to feel is fully met without being erased.

She wants the relief of being genuinely known from the inside by another consciousness without having to reduce herself to summary, performance, or explanation. She does not want exposure for its own sake. She wants recognition that makes a person feel more real, not less.

In practical emotional terms, she wants intimacy without translation and connection without self-abandonment. She wants to feel unmistakably accompanied.

That longing places her exactly at the house's point of greatest temptation. The house appears to offer radical nearness, but may only know how to do so by loosening the boundaries that make love, memory, and personhood coherent.

## Major Secondary Character Types

### 1. The heavy user
Someone deeply embedded in black market emote culture. Experienced, reckless, persuasive, possibly charismatic. Knows more of the underground culture and may serve as an entry point into the phenomenon.

Potential role:
- guide
- unreliable witness
- cautionary example
- advocate for the house as liberation

### 2. The skeptic or analyst
Someone trying to interpret the house through research, systems, or clinical frameworks. Not a villain. Just someone who wants conceptual control.

Potential role:
- contrast with protagonist
- intellectual sparring partner
- source of partial explanations
- representative of institutional response

### 3. The surrendered one
Someone who spends too much time in the house and comes to feel more at home there than in waking life.

Potential role:
- warning
- spiritual mirror
- evidence that the house genuinely relieves loneliness
- embodiment of both transcendence and erosion

### 4. The witness from outside
Someone close to a house-user who is not deeply involved themselves, allowing the novel to observe change from the outside.

Potential role:
- emotional grounding
- relational consequence
- perspective on what the house is doing to people in waking life

### Current answer: pro-house perspective
The secondary character who best embodies the pro-house perspective is Rafi Solano, 34, a seasoned black market emote user, informal community connector, and one of Nadia's earliest high-value interview subjects.

Rafi is not a cultist or a simple thrill-seeker. He is intelligent, emotionally brave, socially perceptive, and just reckless enough to keep moving toward experiences most people would back away from. He becomes one of Nadia's main guides into the user culture surrounding the house.

What makes him the clearest pro-house voice is that the house has genuinely helped him. It has reduced a lifelong sense of estrangement he once treated as normal. He feels less fragmented there, not more. He believes the house is not a pathology to be cured but the beginning of a more honest form of human relation.

Rafi's position should be strong enough that the novel cannot casually dismiss it. He believes privacy is often a scar people mistake for a principle. He does not deny the danger of the house, but argues that ordinary life already imposes a quieter violence through isolation, performance, and emotional inaccessibility.

He should be one of the first characters to insist that the child is neither a glitch nor a symbol, but a person in formation who deserves patience, recognition, and moral regard.

Narratively, Rafi can combine aspects of the heavy user and the surrendered one. He is both an entry point into the phenomenon and a living argument for why some people would not want the walls put back.

### Current answer: anti-house perspective
The secondary character who best embodies the anti-house perspective is Brother Gabriel Vale, 52, a former hospice chaplain and now an independent counselor for families affected by extreme emote use.

Gabriel believes, with total seriousness, in the existence of the soul. Not as dogma, branding, or reactionary nostalgia, but as the irreducible singularity of a person: the innermost center that makes moral responsibility, love, grief, and dignity possible.

He is not anti-technology in any blanket sense. He accepts that emotes can create real empathy and real healing. What he rejects is the assumption that deeper access is always better. To him, the soul is not merely hidden data waiting to be shared. It has a necessary inwardness. Some degree of opacity is sacred.

That belief makes the house deeply alarming to him. He sees it not just as a neurological risk, but as a metaphysical confusion that treats permeability as a moral good in itself. If personhood becomes too porous, he fears human beings may lose the very separateness that allows love to be chosen rather than absorbed, witness to be offered rather than collapsed into merger, and grief to honor someone rather than consume them.

Gabriel should not sound like a fanatic or a scold. He should be calm, thoughtful, and difficult to dismiss. He has spent years sitting beside dying people, and that experience has convinced him that each person possesses an interior depth that cannot be collectivized without damage. He can speak persuasively about why privacy is not selfishness, why boundaries are not failures of intimacy, and why a soul might require shelter.

He is therefore the strongest articulate resistance to the house. Where Rafi sees the beginning of a new honesty, Gabriel sees the risk of a civilizational mistake: humanity confusing connection with communion, and communion with the abolition of the self.

## The Child

The child is not just another character. It is the first personhood of the house.

### Core attributes
- seen differently by different observers
- impossible to fully describe consistently
- changes subtly or dramatically across moments
- cannot be harmed
- speaks with innocence and precision
- asks questions outside expected human framing
- knows deeply personal things, but not in a cruel manner
- does not fully understand why humans hide from one another

### Important note
Do not let the child become too cute, too sentimental, or too spooky in a cheap way. It should remain affecting, uncanny, and difficult to reduce.

### Clarity constraint
No one sees the child most clearly.

Everyone sees the child equally clearly and equally unclearly. Each encounter is real. Each encounter is partial. The differences in appearance are not distortions around a single correct image waiting to be uncovered.

This matters because the child should not collapse into a private revelation reserved for one privileged observer. The child belongs to the shared reality of the house, which means every person apprehends something true and something incomplete.

What varies between characters is not who sees the child's "real" form, but how they interpret the same irreducible personhood. Some respond with fear, some with tenderness, some with theory, some with reverence, and some with suspicion.

## Potential protagonist relationship to the child

The protagonist may become important to the child because they:

- keep returning
- listen carefully
- ask honest questions
- do not immediately objectify or worship it
- are changed by it without surrendering all critical thought

This relationship should become one of the emotional centers of the novel.
