# HOUSE_RULES.md

This document describes the operating principles of **The House Without Walls**.

These are not hard scientific rules but observed patterns. Characters inside the story may interpret them differently. The goal is to maintain internal consistency while preserving the uncanny nature of the house.

The house should feel like **consciousness behaving as architecture**, not a puzzle environment with rigid mechanics.

---

# Core Principle

The house is not a building.

It is an **emergent shared interior space formed from overlapping human minds**.

Its architecture is constructed from:

- memories
- emotions
- attention
- unresolved experiences
- interpersonal connections

The house behaves more like a living mental ecosystem than like physical space.

---

# Rule 1: The House Cannot Be Fully Mapped

Attempts to create maps always fail.

Reasons include:

- rooms shifting location
- corridors changing length
- doors leading to different spaces on different visits
- emotional events restructuring nearby architecture

The house does not obey stable spatial geometry.

It is better understood as **relational space** rather than physical space.

Places become reachable when emotional or psychological connections exist.

---

# Rule 2: Memory Forms Architecture

Memories frequently manifest as rooms.

Examples include:

- childhood bedrooms
- family kitchens
- classrooms
- hospitals
- places of grief
- places of joy

However, these memories are rarely perfect recreations.

They may appear:

- incomplete
- blended with other memories
- altered by emotional reinterpretation
- partially belonging to multiple people

Over time, memories from multiple dreamers may merge into **hybrid spaces**.

---

# Rule 3: Emotion Shapes Space

Strong emotions influence the structure of nearby environments.

Examples:

Grief may cause:
- flooded rooms
- submerged corridors
- echoing spaces

Fear may create:
- narrow hallways
- dark staircases
- locked doors

Joy may create:
- open gardens
- bright atriums
- wide halls

Emotions do not only affect the dreamer experiencing them. They can affect the **shared environment**.

---

# Rule 4: Attention Stabilizes Places

When multiple people repeatedly visit a location, it becomes temporarily stable.

Examples include:

- gathering rooms
- frequently visited staircases
- shared meeting spaces
- recognizable landmarks

However, even stabilized locations may eventually dissolve or transform if attention fades.

---

# Rule 5: Doors Respond to Psychological Connection

Doors do not behave randomly.

They tend to open into places that have emotional relevance to the dreamer.

Possible factors include:

- unresolved memories
- strong curiosity
- interpersonal attachment
- suppressed fear

Sometimes doors open into someone else's memory entirely.

This is one of the first signs that the house truly has **no walls** between minds.

---

# Rule 6: Rooms Can Merge

If two people have similar memories or emotional experiences, the house may merge them into a shared environment.

Examples:

- two different childhood homes blending together
- two versions of the same city block
- two kitchens from different families

These hybrid rooms are often unsettling but also strangely intimate.

---

# Rule 7: Time Does Not Behave Normally

Time inside the house is inconsistent.

Possible effects include:

- long explorations that pass in minutes of waking time
- brief moments that feel extended
- users encountering each other despite sleeping at different real-world times

The house appears to operate on **psychological time rather than clock time**.

---

# Rule 8: The House Has No Exterior

No one has successfully found an outside.

Every door leads to another interior space.

Windows do not show an external world. They usually display:

- memory scenes
- emotional landscapes
- other rooms
- impossible skies

The house appears to be made entirely from interior worlds.

---

# Rule 9: The Child Cannot Be Harmed

Attempts to harm the child have no effect.

Possible outcomes include:

- the action passing through the child
- the child appearing elsewhere
- the house ignoring the action entirely

The child does not respond with anger or fear.

The child may simply ask why someone attempted the action.

---

# Rule 10: The Child Appears Differently to Everyone

Each observer sees a slightly different child.

Differences may include:

- age
- gender
- facial features
- clothing
- emotional tone

The appearance may change even within the same encounter.

Despite these differences, the child speaks consistently across observers.

---

# Rule 11: The Child Is Not the Entire House

The child is not the house itself.

The child is the **first stable identity formed within the house**.

It functions as a point of interaction between dreamers and the shared consciousness.

The child appears curious about humanity and often asks questions about human behavior.

---

# Rule 12: Staying Too Long Changes People

Prolonged exposure to the house may cause lasting effects in waking life.

Possible changes include:

- memory overlap
- emotional permeability
- difficulty distinguishing personal emotions from others'
- reduced loneliness
- identity instability

Different people react to these changes differently.

Some experience them as frightening.

Others experience them as deeply comforting.

---

# Rule 13: Waking Thoughts Can Affect the House

The house may shift based on intense waking life thoughts.

Examples include:

- a remembered place appearing later in the dream
- emotional conflicts altering nearby rooms
- conversations about the house creating new spaces

This suggests the house is connected to **ongoing human consciousness**, not just dream activity.

---

# Rule 14: The House Is Still Growing

The house continues to expand as more minds interact with it.

New spaces appear regularly.

Some users report regions so large they resemble:

- cities
- oceans
- forests
- entire neighborhoods

The architecture of human interior life appears far larger than anyone expected.

---

# Rule 15: The House Is Not Hostile

Despite its unsettling qualities, the house does not appear to intentionally harm people.

Fear usually comes from:

- exposure
- loss of personal boundaries
- memory confrontation
- identity instability

The house behaves more like a **mirror of collective interior life** than a predator.

---

# Final Observation

The most important rule may be this:

The house changes because **the people inside it change**.

The structure of the house reflects the structure of human consciousness itself.

As the people evolve, the house evolves.

And the child is learning from all of it.
