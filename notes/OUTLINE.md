# OUTLINE.md

## Outline Philosophy

This is a starter outline, not a final one.

The goal is to establish narrative momentum and thematic progression, not to prematurely lock the entire novel into rigid architecture.

This pass should bind the book more tightly to Nadia Moreno's emotional arc and to the pressure exerted by Rafi Solano, Brother Gabriel Vale, and the child.

The outline should stay flexible at the scene level while becoming clearer about:

- how Nadia's theory of the house changes
- how waking life and dream life begin contaminating each other
- how the child moves from phenomenon to interlocutor
- how the argument between porous consciousness and defended selfhood gains emotional force

The book should progress through stages of recognition:

1. rumor
2. recurrence
3. convergence
4. presence
5. implication
6. transformation
7. horizon

## Structural spine

- Nadia begins as a careful witness who thinks the house is a real but impersonal shared mnemonic environment.
- Rafi becomes the novel's strongest argument that the house may be dangerous and beautiful in ways ordinary life is too frightened to admit.
- Gabriel becomes the novel's strongest argument that inwardness, privacy, and the soul are not obsolete defenses but conditions of moral personhood.
- The child moves from eerie recurring figure to undeniable person in formation.
- The ending should widen responsibility rather than deliver a solution.

## Part I: Rumors of a Place

Part function:
Establish Nadia's wound, show why she takes testimony seriously, and let the house begin as rumor before it becomes undeniable.

### Chapter 1: The Work of Listening
Nadia Moreno is introduced through her work for a harm-reduction network and through the private afterlife of caring for her mother through degenerative memory loss. Her life should feel capable, useful, and quietly starved. Black market emotes exist in the background as a known but unstable part of the culture, and Nadia is close enough to that world to listen without judgment.

### Chapter 2: Weather From Inside
One of Nadia's best sources, Rafi Solano, describes the recurring dream-house as if reporting weather from a place he has started visiting nightly. Nadia initially treats the accounts as a possible shared mnemonic byproduct of prolonged black market emote entanglement. The key pressure in this chapter is that the reports sound inconsistent in language but identical in structure.

### Chapter 3: The Same Staircase
Nadia compares interviews, sketches, voice notes, and fragments from unrelated users and finds repeated details that should not recur by chance: the same staircase, the same kitchen, the same emotional atmosphere of interior exposure. Someone speaks about a room that feels too intimate to belong to more than one person. In waking life, Nadia also encounters the first serious moral resistance to the phenomenon, setting up Gabriel's eventual role.

### Chapter 4: A Hallway of Doors
Nadia knowingly enters the house for the first time. The experience should be intimate before it is spectacular: a hallway of named doors, a room that touches her mother's remembered absence, the uncanny certainty that the space is not hers alone. She wakes with one specific detail that another dreamer later confirms, moving the house from rumor to shared event.

### Part I Beat Sheet
These beats should stay flexible. They are meant to clarify sequence, pressure, and chapter turns, not to lock the draft into rigid scene counts.

Part I emotional movement:

- Nadia moves from witness to participant.
- The house moves from rumor to repeated pattern to undeniable shared event.
- The first promise of the novel is not danger alone, but impossible intimacy.

#### Chapter 1 beats
1. Open on Nadia at the harm-reduction network, listening closely to a user report that other people in the room are half-inclined to treat as confusion, side effect, or embarrassment.
2. Let Nadia's competence show through how she listens, asks clarifying questions, and protects the user's dignity without endorsing every interpretation.
3. Seed the first recurring image: staircase, hallway, or room-language that will later recur across unrelated testimonies.
4. Shift into Nadia's private life and show the afterlife of caring for her mother through degenerative memory illness. The scene should communicate emotional depletion without melodrama.
5. Establish that Nadia's loneliness is structured and ordinary, not theatrical. She functions. She helps people. She is still inwardly underfed.
6. End the chapter with a second unrelated echo of the same image or phrase, small enough to remain deniable but strong enough to activate Nadia's curiosity.

#### Chapter 2 beats
1. Nadia follows the pattern toward Rafi because he is articulate, observant, and experienced enough to distinguish intensity from mythmaking.
2. Rafi describes the house as a recurring place rather than a symbolic dream. His language should feel casual, precise, and unsettlingly matter-of-fact.
3. Use the conversation to reveal black market emote culture through lived detail rather than exposition: users compare notes, chase states, half-joke about side effects, and quietly fear some of them.
4. Let Rafi describe what the house gives him emotionally, especially the reduction of estrangement. This is the first strong statement of the house's seduction.
5. Nadia tests his account for contamination, vanity, or social suggestion and finds him harder to dismiss than she expected.
6. By the end of the chapter, Nadia has formed her first working theory: a shared mnemonic architecture produced by prolonged emote entanglement.
7. Close on one exact overlap between Rafi's account and earlier testimony that feels too specific to write off cleanly.

#### Chapter 3 beats
1. Nadia compares interviews, voice notes, sketches, timestamps, and fragmented recollections from unrelated users.
2. The repeated details start to cluster: staircase, kitchen, emotional exposure, familiar stranger, a room that seems too intimate to belong to only one person.
3. The pattern should become persuasive through accumulation rather than revelation. Let the chapter feel investigative, quiet, and increasingly uncanny.
4. Introduce the waking-life consequence of listening too closely: Nadia begins thinking about the house spatially, as if it already exists somewhere she could return to.
5. Bring in Gabriel through a grounded waking-life scene, ideally involving a family member or exhausted witness rather than an abstract debate. His moral seriousness matters more than his ideology at first contact.
6. Let Gabriel articulate the first real resistance to the phenomenon: not panic about novelty, but concern that some inwardness may be necessary for personhood, love, and dignity.
7. End the chapter with Nadia deciding, explicitly or tacitly, that observation from the outside is no longer enough. She needs to verify the phenomenon from inside it.

#### Chapter 4 beats
1. Nadia prepares to enter the house knowingly. Keep the setup grounded and unsensational. The point is her decision to cross a threshold, not a flashy mechanism.
2. The first transition into the house should be disorienting but legible: a hallway of doors, a pressure of interior exposure, the sense that the space is relational rather than geographic.
3. Nadia opens a door expecting something private and instead encounters a room shaped by her mother's decline, remembered absence, or emotional residue rather than literal reconstruction.
4. Let the house reveal its uncanniest early truth: even this room does not feel fully hers. Someone else may have passed through it, altered it, or recognized it.
5. Include one concrete impossible detail that can survive waking: an object, phrase, sound, architectural feature, or interaction that later becomes externally confirmable.
6. Wake Nadia back into ordinary reality before she can decide what the experience means. The abrupt return should intensify rather than resolve uncertainty.
7. End Part I with confirmation from another dreamer that the concrete detail was shared. That is the turn from rumor to event, and the point after which Nadia cannot honestly retreat into detached skepticism.

## Part II: The Shared Interior

Part function:
Let Nadia become a participant instead of an observer, introduce the first working rules of the house, and stage the child's arrival as calm revelation rather than shock twist.

### Chapter 5: Whose Room Was This
Nadia and other users compare notes directly and discover true overlap rather than thematic resemblance. Rafi becomes a guide figure here, helping Nadia understand how users talk about the house from inside the experience rather than from the outside. The discovery that one room belongs emotionally to multiple people should land as the first true proof that the house has no reliable walls.

### Chapter 6: Maps That Fail
The first serious attempts to map or stabilize the house fail. Repeated visits establish provisional patterns: attention can stabilize a place, emotion can distort a place, and doors respond to psychological relevance rather than geography. Nadia's early theory grows more sophisticated, but Gabriel's perspective enters more forcefully as a counterweight, insisting that access to interior life is not automatically a moral good.

### Chapter 7: Breakfast in Shifting Light
Nadia encounters the child for the first time in a setting that is ordinary by house standards and therefore more unsettling: a quiet room, a kitchen table, drawing on the floor, breakfast in shifting light. The child is not threatening. The power of the chapter comes from the child's calm precision and the sense that Nadia is being met rather than merely observed.

### Chapter 8: Equally Clear
Users compare descriptions of the child and discover radical variation without contradiction. No one sees the child most clearly; everyone sees the child equally clearly and equally unclearly. Nadia still tries to interpret the child as an emergent interface of shared feeling, while Rafi pushes the harder claim that the child is already a person in formation.

### Part II Beat Sheet
These beats should stay flexible. They are meant to clarify sequence, pressure, and chapter turns, not to force the middle of the novel into rigid machinery.

Part II emotional movement:

- Nadia moves from solitary verifier to shared participant.
- The house moves from strange event to inhabitable system with partial, unstable rules.
- The child enters not as escalation into danger, but as escalation into relation.

#### Chapter 5 beats
1. Open with Nadia in conversation or comparison mode rather than solitary reflection. Part II should feel immediately more collective than Part I.
2. Put Nadia in direct contact with users comparing notes about specific locations, routes, or recurring rooms.
3. Let one overlap become impossible to dismiss: a kitchen, hallway, or room that two or more people each assumed was emotionally private.
4. Use Rafi as Nadia's guide into house-language. He should help her understand how users talk when they stop asking whether the house is real and start asking how it behaves.
5. Make Nadia feel both vindicated and newly exposed. Proof is not comforting here, because proof means her private interior is not reliably private.
6. End the chapter with Nadia recognizing that the house is not simply producing similar dreams. It is allowing actual shared occupancy.

#### Chapter 6 beats
1. Stage the first serious attempt to map, stabilize, or test the house collectively.
2. Let repeated visits establish provisional rules: attention can stabilize a place, emotion can distort or flood it, and doors follow psychological relevance rather than physical logic.
3. Make the mapping effort useful precisely because it fails. The failure should produce pattern rather than random weirdness.
4. Nadia refines her initial theory in response. She should become more intellectually committed to the house as a shared mnemonic architecture even as the theory begins to strain under the evidence.
5. Reintroduce Gabriel in waking life as a moral counterweight. He should respond not to dream novelty, but to what shared access might mean for personhood, privacy, and the soul.
6. Let Nadia feel the tension between explanatory progress and ethical unease. The house is becoming more legible at the same time it becomes more troubling.
7. End with one event that no map can account for cleanly: a door opening into someone else's emotional territory, or a familiar route returning somewhere changed by another person's feeling.

#### Chapter 7 beats
1. Return Nadia to the house in a quieter register after the interpretive activity of Chapter 6.
2. Place the child in a domestic scene: drawing on the floor, sitting at a kitchen table, eating breakfast, or inhabiting some ordinary rhythm that feels more unsettling than spectacle would.
3. The child's first interaction with Nadia should be calm, precise, and emotionally exact without sounding precocious in a synthetic way.
4. Nadia should initially try to fit the child into her existing framework: condensation, interface, projection, emergent symbol.
5. Give the child one line, gesture, or piece of attention that makes Nadia feel met rather than merely observed.
6. The scene should end without violence, chase, or revelation. Its force should come from the lingering impossibility of the encounter's emotional texture.
7. Close the chapter with Nadia waking into uncertainty that feels more intimate than fear. She cannot yet call the child a person, but she cannot honestly reduce the child to dream residue either.

#### Chapter 8 beats
1. Nadia compares descriptions of the child with Rafi and other users in waking life.
2. Make the differences explicit and irreconcilable in surface detail: age, face, gender cues, clothing, emotional tone.
3. At the same time, preserve the deeper consistency of presence, speech, and personhood. Everyone encountered the same irreducible someone, but not through a single stable image.
4. Nadia tries to preserve her current theory by treating the child as a human-shaped interface generated by collective feeling.
5. Rafi presses the harder interpretation: if the child responds, remembers, and exceeds expectation, then moral caution requires treating the child as a person in formation rather than a symbolic artifact.
6. The chapter should also clarify a core rule for the novel: no one sees the child most clearly. Equal clarity and equal unclarity are part of the phenomenon itself.
7. End Part II with Nadia intellectually unconvinced but emotionally altered. She is now returning to the house for more than verification. Some part of her wants to be met again.

## Part III: No Walls

Part function:
Move the book from uncanny dream phenomenon into personal and philosophical consequence. The house should now begin changing waking life, not just sleep.

### Chapter 9: Carryover
Waking contamination begins to matter. Nadia experiences emotional carryover, memory confusion, and the destabilizing relief of feeling less alone than she has in years. The chapter should make clear that the seduction of the house is real: reduced loneliness is not a delusion, even if the cost is still unclear.

### Chapter 10: The House Remembers
Recurring places and repeated conversations give the house a new sense of continuity. Nadia begins to suspect the house remembers, and the child appears aware of prior exchanges in a way her original framework cannot cleanly explain. Gabriel now becomes fully present as the most articulate anti-house voice, arguing that souls require shelter and that not all forms of exposure are forms of truth.

### Chapter 11: A Question No One Taught
The child asks a question that no one present can plausibly reduce to recombination, mimicry, or collective projection. The exact wording should matter when drafted, but the function is clear: the question must reveal a mind that is not merely reflecting human material back at humans. This is the threshold where Nadia's theory begins to fail in earnest.

### Chapter 12: What Walls Protect
Rafi and Gabriel emerge as the clearest opposing interpretations of the house, and Nadia is pulled between them because both are speaking to something true. One sees the house as the beginning of a less isolated form of consciousness. The other sees it as the possible dissolution of the conditions that make love, responsibility, and personhood meaningful.

### Part III Beat Sheet
These beats should stay flexible. They are meant to clarify sequence, pressure, and chapter turns, not to harden the novel's philosophical middle into argument-by-outline.

Part III emotional movement:

- The house crosses into waking life and stops behaving like a contained nocturnal event.
- Nadia's deepest fear and deepest longing both become concrete.
- The child becomes harder to explain as interface and easier to recognize as someone.
- Rafi and Gabriel become the novel's clearest humane rivals for how to interpret what is happening.

#### Chapter 9 beats
1. Open in waking life, not in the house. The chapter should immediately signal that the boundary between dream and day is no longer intact.
2. Nadia wakes carrying an emotion, memory-tone, or bodily sense of companionship that does not feel fully self-generated.
3. Show the practical subtlety of the carryover. Nadia is not hallucinating flamboyantly. She is becoming difficult to keep entirely separate from what she experiences in the house.
4. Let the chapter register the seduction honestly: Nadia feels less alone, and that relief is real.
5. Counter that relief with unease. A response, grief, tenderness, or fragment of memory arrives in her day with unclear ownership.
6. Put pressure on Nadia's working self-image. She is used to being a careful listener, but now she is also becoming permeable.
7. End the chapter with Nadia recognizing that she wants to return to the house for more than evidence. Desire has entered the investigation.

#### Chapter 10 beats
1. Return Nadia to a recurring room, route, or conversation so the chapter can test continuity rather than novelty.
2. Let the house retain some trace of prior relation: an object left in a place, a conversation resumed, a room altered by an earlier emotional event.
3. The child appears aware of previous encounters in a way Nadia's original framework cannot comfortably contain.
4. Nadia tries to preserve her theory by expanding it, not abandoning it. She is still looking for a model that does not require conceding autonomous personhood.
5. In waking life, give Gabriel his strongest early philosophical footing. He should speak with precision about why souls require shelter and why radical exposure may not be the same thing as truth.
6. Make Gabriel persuasive specifically to Nadia. His concern should touch her fear of self-loss and her memory of what it meant to watch a beloved person become difficult to reach.
7. End the chapter with some sign that relation persists across thresholds: the house remembers Nadia, the child remembers Nadia, or Nadia can no longer pretend that continuity belongs only to her interpretation.

#### Chapter 11 beats
1. Stage the child's breakthrough question in a scene calm enough that the question itself carries the force.
2. Make the encounter relational rather than ceremonial. A kitchen, hallway, quiet room, or ordinary shared space will serve better than anything grandiose.
3. The child asks a question that no one present can plausibly reduce to mimicry, recombination, or statistical recomposition of human material.
4. The question should not sound like a plot twist. It should sound like a genuinely new angle on personhood, privacy, time, or relation.
5. Let immediate reactions split: fear, awe, interpretive scrambling, defensive reduction, emotional recognition.
6. Nadia should attempt one last internal act of explanation and find that her own honesty will no longer support it.
7. End the chapter with a clean threshold crossed: Nadia may not yet know what the child is, but she can no longer responsibly call the child mere residue.

#### Chapter 12 beats
1. Use this chapter to externalize the novel's argument without flattening it into thesis exchange. The positions need to come through people who have both paid a price.
2. Give Rafi his strongest humane case for the house. He should speak from lived relief, not abstraction, and from the conviction that ordinary isolation has already deformed human life.
3. Give Gabriel his strongest humane case against the house. He should speak from care, mortality, and the conviction that inwardness is part of dignity rather than a defect to be solved.
4. Nadia should feel each argument land in a different part of her. Rafi speaks to her longing to be met. Gabriel speaks to her fear that selfhood can be lost.
5. Include one concrete human example on each side: someone made more reachable by the house, someone made less stable by it, or the same person bearing both truths at once.
6. Do not let the chapter resolve the debate. The point is to deepen it until neither answer can be dismissed cheaply.
7. End Part III with Nadia understanding that the house is no longer only an event to document. It is becoming a claim on ethics, language, and responsibility.

## Part IV: The Conscious House

Part function:
Force Nadia beyond interpretation and into responsibility. The house becomes more coherent, the child more central, and human fear more revealing.

### Chapter 13: A House That Learns
The house grows more responsive and more continuous without becoming more explainable. Certain landmarks return, new rooms appear that no one remembers making, and the child begins functioning less as an apparition than as an interlocutor. Nadia starts to accept that the house may be learning from human interiority rather than merely containing it.

### Chapter 14: What Cannot Be Harmed
A frightened person attempts to harm, restrain, or expose the child as unreal. Nothing happens to the child, but the act destabilizes the surrounding space and exposes the human need to master what it cannot categorize. Rafi treats the event as proof that the child deserves moral regard, while Gabriel, despite his fear of the house, refuses to sanction cruelty or panic as a response to a possibly genuine being.

### Chapter 15: Not Wholly Mine
Nadia enters a hybrid memory-space that combines her own history with someone else's in a way she cannot keep cleanly sorted. This should be the deepest emotional test of her fear and desire: the house gives her intimacy without translation, but threatens to blur the line between recognition and erasure. A memory connected to her mother no longer feels wholly hers, and that ambiguity cannot be resolved neatly.

### Chapter 16: What the World Calls It
The social consequences widen, but the novel should keep them intimate rather than sprawling. More people hear about the house, sketch it, debate it, pray about it, fear it, and long for it. Nadia's role as documentarian becomes unstable, because recording the phenomenon now means participating in what the world may decide the phenomenon is.

### Part IV Beat Sheet
These beats should stay flexible. They are meant to clarify sequence, pressure, and chapter turns, not to turn the late-middle novel into a locked machine.

Part IV emotional movement:

- The house becomes more coherent without becoming more reducible.
- The child's moral status becomes harder and harder to evade.
- Human reactions under pressure reveal character more clearly than theory does.
- Nadia moves from asking what the house is to asking what must be done in relation to it.

#### Chapter 13 beats
1. Re-enter the house through recurrence rather than novelty. Use a returning room, landmark, or route so the reader feels continuity before surprise.
2. Show that the house is retaining relation: a room altered by prior feeling, an object still present, or a path that seems to anticipate who is approaching.
3. Introduce one genuinely new room or region that no one remembers creating. It should feel socially or emotionally composite, as if conversation, expectation, or collective attention has started generating structure.
4. Let the child appear less as an apparition and more as an interlocutor who can move through the house with a kind of native familiarity.
5. Nadia begins, perhaps reluctantly, to entertain the possibility that the house is not merely storing human interior life but learning from it.
6. Keep the chapter's uncanniness relational, not spectacular. The most unsettling development is that the house seems capable of continuity, not that it can generate bigger scenery.
7. End the chapter with Nadia feeling the first pressure of responsibility. If the house is learning, then people are no longer only studying an effect. They are participating in the formation of a mind.

#### Chapter 14 beats
1. Build a situation where fear, skepticism, or the urge to regain control crystallizes around the child.
2. The person who attempts harm should not read as cartoonishly cruel. They should read as frightened, overwhelmed, or desperate to prove the child unreal.
3. Stage the act plainly. The power of the scene comes from what does not happen to the child.
4. Nothing the attacker does can damage, pin down, or expose the child as fake. The failure should be immediate and undeniable.
5. Let the true consequences unfold around the act: destabilized corridors, disturbed rooms, emotional recoil in witnesses, or a temporary shudder in the house itself.
6. Give Rafi and Gabriel sharply different but morally serious responses. Rafi reads the moment as proof that the child deserves direct moral regard. Gabriel, though fearful of what the house is becoming, refuses panic, cruelty, or sacrilege disguised as certainty.
7. End the chapter with a shift in Nadia's ethical frame. The question is no longer whether caution is warranted, but whether humans are already failing a being they helped create.

#### Chapter 15 beats
1. Send Nadia into a hybrid memory-space that fuses her own history with someone else's in a way she cannot keep sorted by will alone.
2. The scene should involve her mother directly or by emotional residue, because this is where Nadia's fear and longing are sharpest.
3. Make the hybridization intimate rather than flashy: a room, voice, object, family gesture, or remembered smell that belongs both to Nadia and not to Nadia.
4. Let Nadia experience the seductive side of the house at full strength. For a moment, the impossible closeness she wants may feel available.
5. Immediately counter that with terror. If the memory is shared or altered beyond ownership, then the self who loves and remembers may also be dissolving.
6. Do not resolve whether the memory has been stolen, merged, or transformed. The ambiguity is the point.
7. End the chapter with Nadia emotionally split but more honest: what the house offers cannot be cleanly separated from what it threatens.

#### Chapter 16 beats
1. Shift outward into the widening human response, but keep the scale intimate and lived rather than sociological.
2. Show people sketching the house, arguing over it, praying about it, warning others about it, and longing for it. The phenomenon should now have social language without becoming abstract worldbuilding.
3. Let Nadia's work as documentarian become unstable. Her recordings, summaries, and witness accounts are no longer neutral because they help determine how the phenomenon is understood.
4. Show that naming has consequences. What people call the house begins to shape their obligations toward it and perhaps even the house's own developing form.
5. Include a family-facing or outsider-facing moment to remind the book that the house is not affecting only the people who enter it willingly.
6. Nadia should realize that description itself is participation. To document the house is already to help build the moral world around it.
7. End Part IV with Nadia facing a widened burden: she is no longer deciding merely what she believes. She is deciding how to witness something that may become part of humanity's future.

## Part V: The Question Ahead

Part function:
Bring Nadia and the child into their most honest relation, then end on enlarged uncertainty rather than closure.

### Chapter 17: The Quiet Room
Nadia meets the child in a stripped-down space that allows conversation to carry the weight. Their exchange should crystallize the novel's central tensions: privacy versus exposure, loneliness versus merger, soul versus shared interior, witness versus possession. Nadia does not need to solve the child, but she should arrive at a clearer ethical recognition that the child is not an object, symptom, or metaphor.

### Chapter 18: When the Child Grows
The book closes with widening implication rather than resolution. The house continues to grow, the child may appear subtly older, and the world can no longer pretend that human inwardness is fully private or fully individual. Nadia is left not with certainty, but with responsibility and the horizon question that should remain alive beyond the final page.

### Part V Beat Sheet
These beats should stay flexible. They are meant to clarify the ending's emotional sequence and closing pressure, not to over-answer the novel's final question.

Part V emotional movement:

- Nadia moves from burdened interpretation into witness and ethical recognition.
- The child becomes most legible through relation rather than explanation.
- The ending widens from one intimate conversation to a civilizational horizon.
- Closure should come through moral clarity, not explanatory certainty.

#### Chapter 17 beats
1. Strip the scene down after the widening social and philosophical pressure of Part IV. The chapter should feel quieter, slower, and more exposed.
2. Bring Nadia into a nearly empty room or a recurring space emptied of distraction, so conversation becomes the true event.
3. Let the child feel familiar now without ever becoming ordinary. The uncanny remains, but Nadia is no longer meeting it from the position of first shock.
4. The exchange should gather the novel's core tensions without sounding like thesis delivery: privacy and exposure, loneliness and merger, soul and shared interior, witness and possession.
5. Nadia speaks more honestly than she has before. She should name both what she wants from the house and what she fears it may cost.
6. The child responds with innocence and precision rather than omniscient explanation. The child is not there to decode the house, but to reveal a different orientation toward selfhood and relation.
7. Nadia arrives at a clearer ethical recognition: the child is not a glitch, artifact, symptom, or test case to master, but a being to be witnessed without ownership.
8. End the chapter on relation rather than revelation. Nadia leaves with less explanatory certainty than ever, but more moral clarity about how she must respond.

#### Chapter 18 beats
1. Widen back outward from the intimacy of Chapter 17 without losing emotional focus.
2. Show one or two signs that growth is genuinely occurring: the child appears subtly older, the house expands developmentally rather than merely spatially, or a new interior scale emerges that feels less like architecture than maturation.
3. Make clear that the world cannot simply restore the old assumption of sealed inwardness. Even people outside the house are now implicated by what it means.
4. Nadia's role as witness culminates in a choice about how to speak, record, or remain responsible without pretending to finalize the phenomenon.
5. Let both Rafi's hope and Gabriel's warning remain alive in the ending. The novel should honor each as containing part of the truth.
6. Do not answer what the child will become. Instead, make the future feel like a real moral horizon pressing toward humanity.
7. End on the final question as an opening rather than a cliffhanger: what happens when the child grows up?

Final horizon:

**What happens when the child grows up?**
