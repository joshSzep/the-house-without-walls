# OUTLINE.md

## Outline Philosophy

This is a starter outline, not a final one.

The goal is to establish narrative momentum and thematic progression, not to prematurely lock the entire novel into rigid architecture.

The book should progress through stages of recognition:

1. rumor
2. recurrence
3. convergence
4. presence
5. implication
6. transformation
7. horizon

## Part I: Rumors of a Place

### Chapter 1
Introduce protagonist and the emotional condition of their life. Establish loneliness, unresolved interior pressure, or some form of psychic vulnerability. Introduce the cultural background of black market emotes without infodumping.

### Chapter 2
First report of the shared dream phenomenon. It sounds like urban legend, a side effect, or underground folklore. The idea of "a house" is mentioned.

### Chapter 3
The protagonist encounters multiple inconsistent but strangely overlapping accounts. Impossible similarities emerge. A certain room, staircase, sound, or phrase repeats across witnesses.

### Chapter 4
The protagonist enters the house for the first time, or first knowingly realizes they have entered it. The experience is disorienting, intimate, and impossible to process cleanly.

## Part II: The Shared Interior

### Chapter 5
Users compare notes. They discover not just similarity, but actual overlap. One person describes a room another person believed was theirs alone.

### Chapter 6
The house's impossible logic becomes clearer. Mapping attempts fail. The architecture changes between visits. Emotional conditions seem to alter space.

### Chapter 7
The protagonist encounters the child for the first time. The encounter is not violent. It is uncanny, calm, and unforgettable.

### Chapter 8
Different users describe the child differently. Contradictions emerge. The child cannot be stabilized into a fixed appearance or identity.

## Part III: No Walls

### Chapter 9
Users begin experiencing waking-life consequences. Memory confusion. Emotional carryover. Reduced loneliness. Subtle destabilization of identity.

### Chapter 10
The protagonist deepens their relationship with the house. They begin recognizing that the house is not just reactive. It is developing continuity.

### Chapter 11
The child asks a question no user had ever thought. This marks the threshold where the phenomenon can no longer be understood as mere projection or collective hallucination.

### Chapter 12
Competing interpretations emerge. Some see the house as a dangerous collapse of individuality. Others see it as the beginning of a new stage of human consciousness.

## Part IV: The Conscious House

### Chapter 13
The house grows more responsive, more coherent in certain ways, yet no less impossible to map. The child becomes more central as an interlocutor.

### Chapter 14
Someone attempts to harm the child, restrain it, or prove that it is only projection. Nothing happens. The effort reveals more about human fear than about the child.

### Chapter 15
The protagonist has a major encounter within a hybrid memory-space that destabilizes their understanding of personal identity. A memory no longer feels wholly theirs.

### Chapter 16
The social consequences widen. The house is no longer a rumor among a few users. Debate, fear, longing, and reinterpretation spread.

## Part V: The Question Ahead

### Chapter 17
The protagonist confronts the child, or sits with it in a more honest way than before. Their dialogue crystallizes the novel's core questions about selfhood, privacy, loneliness, and shared interior life.

### Chapter 18
The book closes not on a neat answer, but on the widening realization that the child may not remain a child forever.

Final horizon:

**What happens when the child grows up?**

## Expansion Notes

This outline should eventually be expanded into:

- more detailed beat-by-beat scenes
- character-specific arcs
- recurring motifs
- social and institutional consequences
- house-specific recurring locations or patterns
- the protagonist's changing inner life

## Important caution

Do not over-plot this too early.

This book's power will depend heavily on atmosphere, voice, emotional precision, and philosophical accumulation. A mechanically clever outline alone will not save it.
