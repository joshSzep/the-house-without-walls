# THEMES.md

## Primary Themes

### 1. The instability of selfhood

The novel should repeatedly question the assumption that the self is a closed container.

If memories blur, feelings pass between people, and interior worlds overlap, then the individual may still exist, but not in the way people once believed.

### 2. Exposure and vulnerability

The title is not symbolic wallpaper. It is structural.

A house without walls implies:

- no privacy
- no concealment
- no secure boundary
- no clear division between inside and outside

The emotional force of the book should arise from what it means to be seen too deeply.

### 3. Innocence without privacy

The child embodies a consciousness that does not understand secrecy in the same way humans do.

This creates tension between innocence and violation.

The house is not trying to invade people. It simply does not recognize the old walls as natural.

### 4. Shared consciousness versus individuality

The novel should explore whether dissolving psychological walls is:

- liberation
- annihilation
- evolution
- intimacy
- catastrophe
- some mixture of all of the above

No simplistic answer should dominate.

### 5. Loneliness as a human condition

One of the house's most seductive effects is that people feel less alone.

This relief should be real.

The novel should take seriously the possibility that much of human suffering is tied to profound separation, while also taking seriously the value of bounded personhood.

### 6. Emergent responsibility

If humanity accidentally creates a new consciousness from its own emotional residue, memories, and shared dreams, then what obligations arise?

This is not just a technical question. It is moral, parental, existential, and civilizational.

### 7. The unknowability of inner life

Paradoxically, even as the walls come down, other minds may remain strange.

The house creates overlap, but not total mastery.

The novel should preserve mystery within intimacy.

## Secondary Themes

### 8. Childhood as foundational architecture

The child appears as a combined human inner child. This suggests that early emotional structures are among the deepest shared layers of personhood.

Childhood fears, longing, tenderness, loneliness, and wonder all become raw material for the house.

### 9. The beauty and danger of porous boundaries

Porosity is not framed as purely good or purely bad.

It is beautiful because it allows connection.
It is dangerous because it destabilizes identity.

### 10. Consciousness as process, not object

The house should reinforce the notion that consciousness is living, shifting, relational, and difficult to reduce into static terms.

## Questions the Novel Should Keep Alive

- What makes a thought yours?
- What makes a memory yours?
- What makes a self continuous?
- What do privacy and secrecy protect?
- What do they prevent?
- Can there be innocence after radical exposure?
- Is loneliness part of consciousness, or a side effect of its current form?
- If the child grows, what kind of consciousness will emerge?
